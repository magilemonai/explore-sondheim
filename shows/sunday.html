02 — Dots become a line

Pointillism, in sound

Up close, Seurat is dots; step back and they fuse into a figure. Sondheim does the same with rhythm: a rapid, staccato accompaniment of separate notes that the ear blends into a shape. Slide from detached to connected and hear dabs turn into melody. Listen, too, for the figure's third flickering between minor and major (D♭ and D♮) — the same color trick waiting in the next room.

“Sondheim … created a sense of pointillism through rhythm, composing a staccato accompaniment for Georges as he paints.” — The Art Institute of Chicago, which owns the painting

05 — The lyric as paint

Words laid down like dots

The title song barely describes a scene. It mostly names colors — the same handful, reshuffled. Hover a color and watch it land on the canvas. The lyric isn't about the painting; it is the painting.

06 — The finale

“Sunday”

The figures stop quarrelling. George raises a hand. The chatter resolves into a slow, rising chorale, and the painting comes whole. This is an evocation of that build — the canvas filling with light.
